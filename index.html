Voltage Clamp Simulator

This simulator models the Voltage Clamp Experiment explored by Hodgkin and Huxley. Enter a clamped voltage and observe how the conductance of each ion channel changes!

Why is this is important?
Before Hodgkin and Huxley, scientists thought that conductance was constant for ion channels. However, by measuring the membrane current at different clamped voltages, Hodgkin and Huxley calculated that conductance was not the same for each trial!

This discovery revealed that channels are dynamic systems, able to open, close and regulate flow with discretion rather than merely going by concentration gradient.

This gave biologists the first understandings of how electricity was managed in the body, and went on to explain the function of action potentials: an important electrical process in all bodily cells.

Why are sodium channels bulls, and potassium channels sloths?

You might notice that the sodium channel rises, before conductance dies back down. This is because sodium channels open before potassium channels, pulling in an influx of sodium to balance clamped voltage. The sodium channel also has a secondary gate, blocking the uptake of sodium once the concentration within is sufficient.

All that sodium depolarizes the cell, meaning that the cell becomes more positively charged. This depolarization is what creates an action potential! Because sodium channels move fast, we call them bulls.

However, the cell can't keep that charge forever! This is where potassium channels come in: they open slower, allowing potassium to trickle out and rebalance the cell potential. This "resets" the cell and returns it to its resting voltage, which is usually -70 mV. Because of their slow nature, we call potassium channels sloths!

Remember: Conductance is important because it measures how many channels are open. Higher conductance means more ions can move in or out!

Membrane Potential Simulator

This tool lets you customize concentrations of Na⁺, K⁺ and Cl⁻ ions inside and outside a cell membrane. Using the Goldman–Hodgkin–Katz equation, you can yield a membrane potential! You can also check if your membrane potential matches the voltage required for an action potential.

Why is this is important?
A cell's membrane potential measures the difference between the voltage inside and outside of the membrane. When this reaches a critical voltage, (which you can find with this tool!) an action potential is generated!

What is an action potential?

An action potential is an electrical event within the cell which causes certain events to happen, like the opening or closing of certain channels, release of chemicals and neuotransmitters, and more. Because electricity usually does not move directly from cell to cell, it's these secondary events that allow cells to relay an action potential down a line.
